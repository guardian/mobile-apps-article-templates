After the Rehearsal/Persona review – Bergman double bill storms on to stage

Barbican, London
The Swedish auteur’s tales of actors are directed by Ivo van Hove at his most lucid and ostentatious, featuring fine performances and a pulsating tempest

★★★★☆ / ★★★☆☆

‘Can you be two different people at the same time?” asks a character in Persona. The same question might be posed of the show’s ubiquitous Belgian director, Ivo van Hove. We know from his productions of A View from the Bridge and Hedda Gabler that he can do work of concentrated power. He can also, as proved by his versions of Lazarus and Obsession, come across as showily aesthetic. Both sides of his work are visible in this double-bill, performed by Toneelgroep Amsterdam in Dutch (with English surtitles) and drawn from the films of Ingmar Bergman.

After the Rehearsal was made for TV in 1984 and Persona for the cinema in 1966 but the two works have a lot in common. Both are chamber pieces. Both show the influence on Bergman of the Swedish dramatist August Strindberg. Both also raise fascinating questions about the nature of the creative impulse and the mystery of human personality.

But After the Rehearsal, because it is less familiar and because it is about theatre itself, makes a far stronger impact than Van Hove’s rather strenuous attempt to recapture the claustrophobic power of Persona.

The first play, as its title implies, takes place on an empty stage where a director, Hendrik Vogler, sits quietly brooding. He is interrupted by a young actor, Anna, who is playing the semi-divine Agnes in his forthcoming production of Strindberg’s A Dream Play.

Hendrik lectures Anna about theatre, but is haunted by the memory of her mother, Rachel, a celebrated actor with whom he had an affair. As he and Anna fantasise about what might happen if they too became partners, Hendrik realises he is an old man whose life is played out.

Palpably autobiographical, this is an extraordinary piece not only about the corrosiveness of time – “Are you and Papa the same age?” Anna mockingly asks Hendrik just as he is coming on to her – but also about the power of the past. Hendrik considers theatre a place where all of the passions once enacted are secretly stored, which is why the image of his ex-lover comes so readily to mind. Van Hove also respects Hendrik’s dictum that “the actor, the word and the spectator” are the essential ingredients of theatre and gives us one of his purest, most lucid productions, beautifully played by Gijs Scholten van Aschat as Hendrik, Gaite Jansen as Anna and Marieke Heebink as Rachel.

The same three actors also appear in Persona, which strikes me as one of Bergman’s irreclaimable masterpieces. Heebink here plays a famous actor, Elisabeth Vogler, who has retreated into silence after a performance of Electra. Jansen is the young nurse, Alma, who tries to coax Elisabeth back to life by revealing her own story. Scholten van Aschat plays Elisabeth’s husband who, as the two women’s personalities increasingly merge, addresses Alma as if she were his wife. All three actors, plus Lineke Rijxman as a supervising doctor, are excellent but I found Van Hove’s production tricksy and intrusive in its attempt to compensate for the absence of the camera.

In the cinema, the story is told through relentless close-ups of Liv Ullmann and Bibi Andersson, and through the rugged bleakness of the island of Faro where much of the film was shot. Here Van Hove starts with a prolonged image of a naked Elisabeth stretched out on a slab, which is gratuitous since her problems lie in the mind. To achieve the transition from a hospital to a remote island, the set by Jan Versweyveld does a spectacular transformation to a water-lapped platform with visible industrial machinery to create a pulsating storm. It is all very clever, but it obscures the fact that this is a psycho-drama about performance, silence and the power of one personality over another.

The real influence on Persona is Strindberg’s The Stronger, in which, as two women meet in a cafe, the talker reveals herself to be weaker than her silent partner. You just about get the point here, and Jansen, through rapt stillness, focuses our attention on Alma’s hypnotic recall of a strange erotic encounter. It was also bizarre to hear the song, Happiness, blaring out of hospital radio: Ken Dodd and Bergman are not names you very often associate.

But, in the end, Persona seems a pointless attempt to turn film into theatre. Although Van Hove shows Heebink and Jansen wearing white slips and with similarly sea-soaked locks, they remain visibly distinct, whereas Bergman, by merging the faces of Ullmann and Andersson, created an unforgettably iconic image. Ivo van Hove, soon to stage Network at the National, is a fitfully brilliant director but, as this Bergman duo proves, at his best when he is being rigorously pure rather than ostentatiously ingenious.

• At the Barbican, London, until 30 September. Box office: 0845-120 7511.
