Land of lines and silence: Emily Shur's Japan – in pictures

Solitary smokers, dogs in the sunshine and stones in the snow are some of the luminous encounters in celebrity portraitist Emily Shur’s journeys across Japan

Tojinbo, Sakai, 2011

American photographer Emily Shur has travelled to Japan 16 times with her camera – and no other aim other than, as she put it, to see what was there. A new publication, her first monograph, catalogues the things that caught her eye. Her book Super Extra Natural! is published by Kehrer Verlag.

All images © Emily Shur

Meiji Memorial Hall, Tokyo, 2012

This project shows an unexpected side to Shur, who, as one of the most sought-after portraitists, has photographed celebrities from Helen Mirren and Lupita Nyong’o to Will Ferrell and Larry David. By contrast, Super Extra Natural! sees her train her lens on an altogether quieter world

Smoker, Shimokitazawa, 2014

Shur, who was born in New York City and grew up in Houston, first visited Japan in 2004. The freedom and peace she found led her to return again and again over a dozen years

Playground, Atami, 2013

‘Everything made sense,’ Shur says of Japan. ‘Lines, shapes, light and colour fit together like a math equation that added up to what still feels like a supernatural high’

Orange Ladder, Tokyo, 2014

The body of work these journeys to Japan produced is both luminous and opaque. Even when photographing seemingly inconsequential details – the base of a ladder on a patch of dirt, say – Shur has an eye for salient colour, graphic shadow and tactile texture

Osaka Station, Osaka, 2015

‘It was clear from the start that [in Japan] I had found my place,’ says Shur. ‘A country whose natural beauty is accentuated by thoughtful design, a culture that values simplicity and kindness, yet also embraces the completely absurd’

Ferry Ride, Seto Inland Sea, 2013

Perhaps because Shur undertook the project with no defined goal – nor itinerary – other than to keep an open mind, the images are infused with a sense of waiting and time slowed down; expectation and frustration in equal measure

Belongings, Enoshima, 2010

If her Japanese explorations were freeing adventures, they also came with strain and fatigue. ‘Maintaining the intoxication of seeing anything with fresh eyes eventually becomes its own challenge,’ says Shur

Photograph: Emily Shur

Hotel Pool, Osaka-fu, 2014

The artist explains that what began as an exploration of an unknown place evolved into an exploration of photography itself, and the act of making pictures

Photograph: Emily Shur

White Van, Miyajima, 2012

‘The place absolutely informs and affects the images, but the images are not about a place,’ says Shur. ‘They’re about a state of mind’

Stone in the Snow, Yamanouchi, 2011

‘I learned to surrender to and embrace the unexpected,’ says Shur. ‘What I thought would be interesting sometimes wasn’t, but some of my favourite images were taken because I went the wrong way or got lost’

Sweet Dog, Kanazawa, 2011

Emily Shur majored in photography at the Tisch School of the Arts at New York University. She lives and works in Los Angeles
