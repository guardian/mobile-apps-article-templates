The Guardian view on English laws: a genuine issue but a partisan plan
Editorial

Published: 23:12 GMT+1 Wednesday, 21 October 2015

Follow Editorial Following Editorial

GA constitutionally constructive response to devolution’s effects on the UK parliament is overdue, but Chris Grayling’s plans do not measure up

The main item on the agenda for the House of Commons on Thursday may seem drily procedural, Westminster-bubble stuff. But the motion “to approve standing orders relating to English votes for English laws”, to be moved by the leader of the Commons, Chris Grayling, plays with constitutional fire. Within the thickets of subclauses and the jungle of parliamentary drafting of these new standing orders lurks a potentially explosive question. Is the Commons becoming the parliament of a state whose constituent nations are now inexorably and antagonistically moving apart from one another?

For a few English voters, English votes for English laws is not a dry phrase. For most Scots, not just nationalists, it certainly isn’t one. David Cameron’s promotion of Evel on the morning after Scotland’s vote to stay in the UK was an incendiary and irresponsible act. In many Scots’ eyes, it instantly undermined the credibility of pro-UK political parties and promises – with consequences that continue to this day. Tory and press fear campaigns against the SNP in the general election added to this further. The standing orders on which MPs will vote on Thursday are not in fact particularly objectionable in themselves. However, in the context of post-referendum distrust they can all too easily be represented, as they are being, as a destructive and hostile partisan act.

Having pledged to introduce Evel in its election manifesto for the third successive time, the Tories nevertheless have a mandate for new proposals. But Mr Grayling’s plans will displease many, even outside Scotland. All such plans are a response to the “West Lothian question”, which asks why Scottish MPs should be able to vote on purely English matters when English MPs can no longer vote on Scottish equivalents. Those who wanted the Conservative pledge to signal the creation of an English parliament will get little from them, beyond the possibility that this is a foot in the Westminster door. Those who hoped Mr Grayling might accept the minimalist and sympathetically organic approach to change, as embodied in the 2013 McKay commission on the parliamentary consequences of devolution, will be disappointed too. Mr Grayling has ended up closer to the latter than to the former. But he is proposing a step into a new sort of parliament all the same.

In principle, plans of this sort have a necessary place, especially necessary in an asymmetric union like the UK. It is right that parliament should better reflect the changes that devolution has created for the UK. It is also right that the specifically English dimension of parliament’s work should be more sympathetically acknowledged. Westminster is, after all, the parliament of the United Kingdom and at the same time the only parliament that the people of England have. The question is whether these proposals deal with the West Lothian question constructively rather than destructively and whether the proposals will have unintended consequences.

Both the SNP and Labour, each contending to be the voice of Scotland, have a strong partisan interest in opposing Mr Grayling’s package. The best approach may yet be to try it and see. The main proposal in Mr Grayling’s plans is to have an English-only Commons “consent stage” written into the legislative process for English-only business. The consent stage would apply to bills, including finance bills, and to secondary legislation in the form of statutory instruments. Without English consent, such business would go no further. All the other existing stages of the process – like the familiar second and third readings, and report – would continue to take place in the whole UK-wide house. The House of Lords would not be affected.

There are practical pitfalls here, some of them deep, and made more so by the current heated atmosphere. In practice, the potential for conflict between English MPs and those from the rest of the UK only seriously exists when the UK government lacks a majority in England. But that is not true today, since the Tories have majorities in both. If, on the other hand, the UK government did not have a majority in England, English MPs could hold a UK government to ransom under these proposals; in that case, these standing orders would be unworkable and would have to be replaced. So, what is the point of them other than to provoke trouble?

It makes sense, as the Commons procedure committee says, to pilot these plans. But there will be no long-term answer to these issues until all sides are willing to approach them in a spirit of constitutional generosity. Right now, that still seems light years away.
