His latest novel is set in an ancient land populated with ogres and pixies, but is it fantasy? The Booker-winning author explains all – and answers questions from a live audience

Kazuo Ishiguro's first full novel in 10 years appears to be a radical departure from his previous work, with its setting of a land where humans live in warrens wreathed in a mist of forgetfulness, and an ageing Sir Galahad wanders the land in search of a dragon to slay.

Ishiguro caused a storm on publication by appearing to suggest that he hoped readers wouldn't take it as fantasy. But what did he really say? In a session staged as part of the Guardian Live programme of events, the Booker-winning author set the record straight.

Jenny Bhatt in response to 31w ago

It's a bit of a shame that the big story about this latest book from one of the best writers of our times is more to do with a silly debate on genre classification than it is to do with the merits of the work itself. I do understand that, now that this particular Pandora's box of "fantasy or not" has been opened, it will remain difficult to avoid getting into it when discussing or reviewing 'The Buried Giant', I suppose only time will tell if this book, too, will prevail for the right reasons and not because of some silly sideshow debate. That said, I appreciate that this podcast was more about allowing the author to set the record straight.

I don't seek out a book because it is a particular genre. I seek it out because of the story and the writing. I'm sure there are many others like me out there. To me, the best kind of book is one that defies any neat, clean genre classification because it successfully blends elements of different genres and, thus, stands apart in its own right.

[Note: I have yet to read the book, but, as I've read and enjoyed most of Ishiguro's work so far, I intend to get to it soon enough.]

MythicalMagpie in response to 31w ago

I was very sorry to miss the live event so thank you for posting the podcast.

The Buried Giant won me over by the end. The sense of Ishiguro's elderly couple's close relationship being built on the ability to forget that which can't be forgiven in their long lives together is incredibly poignant. The idea of forgetting as both a good and a bad thing pervades this novel on both a large and a small scale, but it's the small scale that really hits home.

I also agree with Ishiguro about the fences genre classification puts up in fiction. They limit readers as much as writers. It restricts thought and imagination in a place where the landscape of ideas should be free to wander at will.

MythicalMagpie in response to MythicalMagpie 31w ago

Thinking about it, Ishiguro also manages to convey in The Buried Giant how our role in society changes as we age. As we lose physical strength our value becomes lodged in our memories, in experience and knowledge. If we choose to forget everything as a society then that value is also lost. The elderly would be edged out and marginalised, even more than they are already. It's really a very subtly perceptive novel, not escapist at all.
