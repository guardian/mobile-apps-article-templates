Tom Stoppard says audiences don't get his jokes – is that a hard problem?

The playwright claims he now has to dumb down some lines for his literary references to be understood. But that doesn’t mean audiences are less clever than they used to be

I see Tom Stoppard has been complaining , at an NT Platform event , that audiences don’t always pick up the cultural allusions in his plays. What I find fascinating is that he seizes on literary, rather than scientific or philosophical, references. When the lead character in Stoppard’s new play, The Hard Problem , asks, in the midst of a pilates class, “Could the cosmos be teleological?” we are clearly meant to understand the question. But, according to Stoppard, a glancing reference to King Lear in Travesties is now beyond the average audience’s comprehension.

My first question is “Does it matter?” Any audience is made up of a mixed group of people: some will get an allusion, some not. The context usually makes it clear. When Henry, the hero of Travesties, is told “You were a wonderful Goneril at Eton” it’s pretty obvious that the reference is to a female character.

Shakespeare’s own plays are also packed with now obscure allusions that audiences intuitively understand. Only one person in a hundred will know that when Sir Andrew in Twelfth Night says “ I had as lief be a Brownist as a politician ” he is referring to a breakaway religious sect regarded as seditious by Elizabethans. But the line still gets a laugh, not least because the association of “politician” with “schemer” has a modern ring.

I take Stoppard’s wider point that what constitutes “general knowledge” has changed in his, and my own, lifetime. I’m always struck by the way students on University Challenge tackle everything from higher mathematics to popular music with dazzling ease but often stumble over literary questions. Any journalist will also tell you that you can no longer presume that readers will automatically pick up on bookish allusions. If I were to casually refer to “Mr Gradgrind”, for instance, I would probably have to explain that he was a Dickens character who embodied a belief in the supremacy of facts.

Related: Tom Stoppard: playwright of ideas delivers a new Problem

I would question, however, Stoppard’s assumption that theatre audiences are less clever than they used to be. If anything, they are far cleverer. Plays such as Michael Frayn’s Copenhagen , Timberlake Wertenbaker ’s After Darwin, Complicite’s Mnemonic , Tom Morton-Smith’s Oppenheimer and The Hard Problem itself now assume we will be able to follow complex arguments about everything from nuclear physics to the workings of the human brain. If, in the process, audiences have become less quick at spotting references to classical literature or Shakespeare, that is a small price to pay.

I’d also argue that you don’t have to get everything to enjoy a night in the theatre. A fellow critic, Alastair Macaulay, once wrote very shrewdly that part of the pleasure of watching a Pinter play is not understanding everything. In the same way, I know there are aspects of The Hard Problem I didn’t grasp at a single sitting. But there was enough there, emotionally and intellectually, to give me an enriching evening; and, if some spectators didn’t immediately cotton on to Stoppard’s literary jokes, I frankly – like Rhett Butler in Gone With the Wind – don’t give a damn. Nor should anyone else.
